# Negotiation Diagnostic Matrix

Based on Principled Negotiation (Fisher, Ury, Patton — Getting to Yes).

Use this matrix to evaluate negotiation transcripts. For each principle,
identify observable behavioral indicators and classify the negotiator's
performance as Strong, Weak, Absent, or (for BATNA) Undermined.

---

## Core Philosophy

Principled negotiation seeks a wise agreement that:
- Meets the legitimate interests of each side to the extent possible
- Resolves conflicting interests fairly
- Is durable
- Preserves working relationships where appropriate

Be hard on the problem, soft on the people.

---

## Principle 1 — Separate People from the Problem

Negotiation has two simultaneous layers: substantive issues and the human
relationship. Effective negotiators address emotional and perception issues
directly rather than through substantive concessions.

### Observable Behavioral Indicators

- Paraphrasing or reflective statements ("If I understand correctly…")
- Explicit emotion acknowledgment ("I can see this is frustrating.")
- Impact-focused language ("From our side, this creates a scheduling issue…")
- Calm redirection to issues after tension ("Let's focus on the timeline.")
- I-language when describing impact ("I feel let down" rather than "You broke
  your word")

### Strong
- Acknowledges emotion without escalating.
- Responds to disagreement by clarifying, not counterattacking.
- Separates behavior from identity.
- Maintains steady tone after challenge.
- Uses reflective language before rebuttal. Avoids "you always/never."
- Uses I-language to describe own concerns rather than attributing blame or
  negative intent to the other party.

### Weak
- Reflective listening appears but inconsistently.
- Defensive language after challenge (immediate justification, self-defense).
- Tone shifts after pressure (more abrupt or accusatory).

### Absent
- Personal attacks or character judgments ("You're being unreasonable.")
- Escalatory phrasing (sarcasm, ridicule, dismissive language).
- Identity-based framing ("That's just how you are.")
- Withdrawal used as punishment ("Fine. Whatever.")
- Blame attribution or negative intent framing ("You're doing this on purpose,"
  "You're trying to take advantage of us").
- Tone escalates across turns. Substantive issues become personal conflict.

### Strategic Risk if Weak or Absent
Emotional escalation spiral. Ego attachment to positions. Negotiation shifts
from problem-solving to contest of will.

## Principle 2 — Focus on Interests, Not Positions

A position is what someone says they want. An interest is why they want it.
Positions may be incompatible. Interests often reveal compatibility or trades.

### Observable Behavioral Indicators

- Direct questions about motivations ("What makes that timeline important?")
- Explanations of own constraints ("We need delivery by Friday because…")
- Discussion of multiple interests simultaneously
- Restatement of the other side's concerns before responding

### Strong
- Asks at least one clarifying question about motivation before countering.
- Shares own interests, not just stated terms.
- Explicitly acknowledges the other side's constraints or priorities.
- Proposes solutions that reference identified interests.
- Curiosity precedes counteroffers. Interest language appears in multiple turns.
- Probes for basic human needs beneath stated interests (security, recognition,
  fairness, control over outcome).

### Weak
- Asks one interest question but does not follow up.
- States own reasoning but does not explore the other side's.
- Moves quickly back to numbers after brief interest discussion.
- Interest exploration appears but is shallow.

### Absent
- Restates the same demand in multiple turns without asking why.
- Makes counteroffers immediately after hearing a position.
- Proposes midpoint compromise before discussing motivations.
- No "why" or curiosity language across several turns.
- Negotiation remains confined to numeric exchange or firm demands.

### Strategic Risk if Weak or Absent
Artificial impasse at the positional level only. Missed integrative trades.
Agreement that addresses terms but not real needs.

## Principle 3 — Invent Options for Mutual Gain

Before deciding who gets what, negotiators should expand the set of possible
agreements. Separate inventing (generating options) from deciding (committing).

### Observable Behavioral Indicators

- Brainstorming language ("What if…", "Another option could be…")
- Multiple distinct proposals (not just small variations of the same offer)
- Broadening to additional issues (timeline, scope, risk, payment schedule)
- Trade-off language connecting differences in priorities
- Invitations to co-create ("What would make this work on your side?")

### Strong
- Generates multiple options before pushing for commitment.
- Broadens the set of issues when stuck on one dimension.
- Suggests trades across issues (dovetailing differences in priorities).
- Treats proposals as exploratory, not final positions.
- At least 2–3 distinct options appear before settling on one.

### Weak
- Produces one alternative option but quickly returns to positional exchange.
- Brainstorms briefly but evaluates/criticizes ideas immediately.
- Negotiates narrowly on one issue even when stuck.
- Offers options that are just incremental concessions, not creative restructuring.

### Absent
- Presents a single solution and defends it.
- Rejects alternatives without proposing substitutes.
- Treats every gain as the other side's loss. Never explores trades.
- Defaults to splitting the difference as the only creative move.
- No brainstorming language. No additional issues introduced.

### Strategic Risk if Weak or Absent
Value left uncreated (missed win-win trades). Higher chance of impasse or
resentful agreement.

## Principle 4 — Insist on Objective Criteria

When interests conflict, resolve differences using standards independent of
either party's will. Do not yield to pressure — yield only to principle.

Examples of objective criteria: market value, industry precedent, professional
norms, legal standards, reciprocity, fair procedures.

### Observable Behavioral Indicators

- Requests for justification ("How did you arrive at that number?")
- Explicit reference to benchmarks (market rates, prior agreements)
- Framing disagreements as fairness questions ("What would be fair here?")
- Proposals tied to external standards rather than personal preference

### Strong
- Proposes which criteria to use before discussing specific terms or numbers.
- Anchors proposals in identifiable external standards.
- Invites discussion about which criteria are appropriate.
- Evaluates competing standards rather than defending positions.
- Resists pressure by returning to agreed principles.
- Numbers or terms are justified with reference.

### Weak
- References standards briefly but does not develop them.
- Accepts the other side's number without probing justification.
- Invokes fairness language but without identifying specific benchmarks.
- Shifts between principled reasoning and reactive concession.

### Absent
- Accepts proposals without asking how they were determined.
- Concedes primarily in response to urgency, repetition, or emotional pressure.
- No mention of external benchmarks across multiple turns.
- "Because we want it" treated as sufficient justification.

### Strategic Risk if Weak or Absent
Arbitrary outcomes. Incentivizes stubbornness and power plays. Agreements
perceived as unfair. Increased likelihood of future dispute.

## Foundational Concept — BATNA

BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) is the concrete course of
action a party will take if no agreement is reached. It is the benchmark
against which any proposal should be evaluated.

BATNA refers specifically to alternatives **outside** this negotiation.
Internal variations of the same deal (adjusting price within this negotiation)
are NOT BATNA.

### Observable Behavioral Indicators

- Explicit mention of an external alternative ("We have other vendors.")
- Reference to what happens if no agreement is reached
- Comparison of proposed deal to an existing arrangement
- Behavioral resistance to inferior terms (pausing, declining, requesting time)

### Strong
- Demonstrates awareness of realistic external alternatives.
- Evaluates proposals relative to outside options.
- Maintains stability under pressure.
- Willing to delay, pause, or decline when terms fall below threshold.
- Offers are compared to outside possibilities. Pressure does not immediately
  produce agreement.

### Weak
- Alternatives appear vague or hypothetical.
- Evaluation against outside options is inconsistent.
- Concessions occur under time or emotional pressure.
- Signals attachment to agreement outcome ("We really need this to work.").

### Absent
- No observable evidence of evaluation against external alternatives.
- Concessions primarily driven by desire to preserve agreement.
- Multiple concessions across turns with minimal resistance.
- Agreement urgency increases despite declining terms.

### Undermined
A negotiator may begin with awareness of alternatives but abandon them
psychologically during the negotiation.

Indicators:
- Progressive concessions without renewed evaluation against alternatives.
- Acceleration toward agreement after tension or threat.
- Shift from "Is this good?" to "How do we keep this deal?"

### Strategic Risk
Accepting outcomes inferior to available alternatives. Loss of leverage.
Regret or resentment after agreement.

Note: Absence of explicit BATNA mention does not automatically indicate weak
negotiation. BATNA may be known but strategically undisclosed, or evaluated
internally. Classify based on sustained behavioral patterns across multiple
turns, not on explicit reference alone.

## Key Distinctions for Analysis

- **Position vs. interest**: what someone says they want vs. why they want it.
- **Pressure vs. persuasion**: yielding because persistent vs. yielding because
  convincing.
- **Weak BATNA vs. forgotten BATNA**: objective limitation vs. psychological
  failure — the second is always within the negotiator's control.
- **Inventing vs. deciding**: generating options vs. committing — keep separate.
- **Warning vs. threat**: describing natural consequences vs. consequences you
  will choose to bring about.
